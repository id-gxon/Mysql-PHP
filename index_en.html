Vincent van Gogh

Vincent Willem van Gogh(30 March 1853 â€“ 29 July 1890) was a Dutch Post-Impressionist painter who posthumously became one of the most famous and influential figures in Western art history. In a decade, he created about 2,100 artworks, including around 860 oil paintings, most of which date from the last two years of his life. They include landscapes, still lifes, portraits and self-portraits, and are characterised by bold colours and dramatic, impulsive and expressive brushwork that contributed to the foundations of modern art. Not commercially successful, he struggled with severe depression and poverty, eventually leading to his suicide at age thirty-seven. Van Gogh was considered a madman and failure throughout his life. He, who only became famous after suicide, was seen as a misunderstood genius in the public's imagination, and his reputation grew in the early 20th century when elements of his style were integrated by the Beast and German expressionists. Today, his legacy is celebrated in his name by the Van Gogh Museum in Amsterdam, which houses the world's largest collection of his paintings and paintings.
